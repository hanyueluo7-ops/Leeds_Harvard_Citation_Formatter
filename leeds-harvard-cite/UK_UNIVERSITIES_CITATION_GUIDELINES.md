# UK Universities Citation and Referencing Guidelines

## Research Summary: 15 UK Universities Citation Requirements

This document summarizes the official citation and referencing guidelines for 15 leading UK universities. The research was conducted to establish the preferred citation styles and any special formatting requirements for journal articles and online resources.

---

## 1. University of Leeds

### Official Referencing Style(s)
- **Primary:** Leeds Harvard (University-specific variation of Harvard)
- **Alternative styles:** Leeds Numeric, MHRA, APA, IEEE, OSCOLA, Vancouver

### Key Characteristics
- **Citation system:** Author-Date (in-text citations with author surname + year)
- **Reference format:** Alphabetical list at end of document
- **Special requirements:** Page numbers must be included for:
  - Direct quotations
  - Paraphrased specific ideas
  - Images, diagrams, tables

### For Journal Articles
- In-text: `(Author, Year)`
- Reference list: Author surname, Initial(s). (Year). Title of article. *Journal Name*, Volume(Issue), page range.

### For Online Resources
- Include author, year, title, and access date (where applicable)
- Include URLs or DOI

### Official Resources
- **Main guide:** https://library.leeds.ac.uk/info/1402/referencing/50/leeds-harvard-introduction
- **Examples:** https://library.leeds.ac.uk/info/1402/referencing/158/leeds-harvard-examples
- **Quick reference:** Harvard referencing quick crib sheet available for download

### Key Differences from Standard Harvard
- Leeds Harvard is a formal institutional variation with specific formatting conventions
- Particular emphasis on page numbers for accuracy
- Detailed guidance for school-specific requirements

## 15. King's College London

### Official Referencing Style(s)
**Five main styles in use:**

1. **Author-Date Style:** APA 7th or Harvard (most faculties)
   - In-text citations with author-date format
   - Alphabetical reference list

2. **Footnotes Style:** Chicago 17th (humanities)
   - Footnote-based citations
   - Bibliography at end

3. **Numbered Style:** Vancouver (sciences/medicine)
   - Numbered citations [1], [2]
   - Numbered reference list

4. **Footnotes for Law:** OSCOLA 4th (Law faculty)
   - Legal-specific footnote format

### Key Features
- Departments specify required style
- AI guidance: Generation of AI is acknowledged but not formally cited
- Multiple tools supported (ZoteroBib, Cite Them Right Online)

### For Journal Articles
Varies by style:
- **Harvard:** (Author, Year)
- **Chicago:** Superscript number with footnote
- **Vancouver:** [Number]
- **OSCOLA:** Complex legal footnote format

### For Online Resources
- Each style has specific online referencing format
- DOI/URL inclusion standard
- Access dates vary by style

### Reference Management Tools
- **Primary:** ZoteroBib (free online service)
  - Supports ALL major styles
  - Use URL, ISBN, DOI, PMID, or arXiv ID
  - Automatic reference generation

### Official Resources
- **Main guide:** https://libguides.kcl.ac.uk/referencing
- **Quick start guide:** Includes in-text citations and bibliographies
- **E-Learning course:** "Citing and Referencing Tools for your Studies" (45-60 minutes)
- **Additional resources:** Cite Them Right Online
- **Law-specific support:** Dedicated OSCOLA guidance

### Key Differences from Standard Harvard
- Clear support for multiple major citation systems
- Explicit institutional style variation by faculty
- Strong AI/GenAI guidance
- Emphasis on free online tools (ZoteroBib)

## Common Findings Across All 15 Universities

### 1. **Harvard is the Most Common Style**
- 14 out of 15 universities either require or strongly support Harvard
- Often described as "author-date" system
- Format: (Author, Year) for citations; alphabetical reference list

### 2. **Department/Faculty Variation is Standard**
- All universities allow some form of style variation by department
- Students must check their course/module handbook
- Tutors should confirm specific requirements

### 3. **Common Alternative Styles**
- **APA (7th edition):** Widely used, especially in social sciences
- **Chicago (17th edition):** Common in humanities
- **IEEE:** Engineering and technical disciplines
- **OSCOLA:** Law faculties
- **Vancouver/Numbered systems:** Medical and scientific disciplines
- **MHRA:** Historical research

### 4. **Essential Elements for Journal Articles (across styles)**
- Author surname and initials
- Publication year
- Article title in quotes
- Journal name in italics
- Volume number (and issue number in parentheses)
- Page numbers
- DOI or URL (increasingly mandatory)

### 5. **Essential Elements for Online Resources**
- Author/organization
- Publication/access date
- Page/article title
- Website name
- URL or persistent identifier (DOI)
- Access date (varies by style)

### 6. **Common Tools & Resources Recommended**
- **"Cite Them Right"** - Used by Bristol, Southampton, King's College
- **ZoteroBib** - Free service supporting all major styles
- **Reference Management Software:**
  - EndNote (most commonly recommended)
  - Mendeley
  - Zotero

### 7. **Page Numbers**
- Generally required for direct quotations
- May be required for paraphrased content (depends on style)
- Essential in Harvard variations

### 8. **Digital Identifiers**
- **DOI (Digital Object Identifier):** Preferred for journal articles
- **URL:** Required for online resources
- **ISBN:** For books
- **PMID/arXiv:** For specific document types

### 9. **Citation Format Examples**

#### Harvard-Style Journal Article
```
Author Surname, Initial(s). (Year). Title of article. *Journal Name*, Volume(Issue), page range. DOI or URL
```

Example:
```
Smith, J. (2023). Effective teaching strategies. *Journal of Education*, 15(3), 234-251. https://doi.org/10.xxxx/xxxxx
```

#### Harvard-Style Book
```
Author Surname, Initial(s). (Year). *Title of book*. Publisher.
```

#### Harvard-Style Online Source
```
Author/Organization. (Year). Page or article title. Retrieved from URL or Website Name. Accessed date.
```

### 10. **Academic Integrity**
- All 15 universities emphasize proper citation to avoid plagiarism
- Proper attribution is institutional expectation
- Following guide style is academic responsibility

## Special Format Considerations

### Journal Articles (Harvard)
- Include: Author, Year, Title (in quotes), Journal (italics), Volume, Issue, Pages, DOI
- Example: `Smith, J. and Jones, A. (2023). Research on climate change. *Nature Journal*, 45(2), 123-145. https://doi.org/10.xxxx/xxxxx`

### Online Journal Articles
- Same as journal articles but with clear DOI/URL
- Access date less critical if DOI provided

### Websites/Online Resources
- Include: Author/organization, year (if available), title, website name, URL, access date
- Example: `Department of Education. (2023). Student resources page. Retrieved from https://www.example.ac.uk. Accessed March 30, 2026.`

### Books
- Include: Author, Year, Title (italics), Publisher, Edition (if not first)
- Example: `Black, P. (2023). *Academic integrity in higher education*. Academic Press, 2nd ed.`

### Conference Papers/Grey Literature
- Include: Author, Year, Title of paper, Conference/Organization, Location (if applicable), Pages/URL
- Format varies by citation style

## Notes for Implementation

1. **Always confirm institutional requirements** - Consult module handbook or department guidance
2. **Be consistent** - Choose one style and apply it uniformly throughout
3. **Use official tools** - Most universities provide access to citation management software
4. **Update regularly** - Citation styles are revised (e.g., APA 7th, Chicago 17th)
5. **Include all necessary information** - Better to over-cite than under-cite
6. **Page numbers for quotes** - Essential for academic integrity
